Sara’s Explanation of Propulsion Equations

To understand how MPD thrusters compare to other electric propulsion methods for spacecraft, there are a few key variables whose relationship needs to be understood: Power, Thrust, Efficiency, Specific Impulse (Isp), and delta_V or dV ( change in velocity).

Power is typically measured in watts. You can think of power for a satellite propulsion system similarly to how we think of “horsepower” for car engines. A 10 kW electric thruster is more powerful than a 5 kW thruster. However, power level alone can’t tell us much about how the engine performs. To understand the engine’s ability to perform certain maneuvers or missions, you’ll need to know more about the engine: its thrust level, its efficiency, or its isp.

Thrust is measured in lbf (pounds force). Thrust is easily demonstrated through Newton’s third law: For every action there is an equal and opposite reaction. If you were an astronaut in space and you threw an object away from you, you would be pushed in the opposite direction. You can imagine if that object was very heavy, you would be pushed in the opposite direction with much more force than if it was light. You can also imagine that if you threw that object very slowly, you might not be pushed in the opposite direction very quickly, but if you threw that object very quickly, you would be thrust in the opposite direction even faster. Thrust is equal to how much mass you throw over time * the velocity at which you throw it. Or more specifically, thrust is equivalent to mass flow rate * exit velocity. A higher thrust engine will push your vehicle with more force over a shorter period of time than a lower thrust engine.

*Note: You can also use F for “Force” interchangeably for T for “Thrust” in this situation

Isp stands for “specific impulse”. Specific impulse is similar to the gas mileage for your car. It is a ratio that tells you how efficiently your propulsion system uses propellant. To be specific, Isp tells you how much thrust you get in exchange for a weight flow rate of propellant. Isp = thrust/weight flow rate. Or, broken down even further, Isp = thrust/(mass flow rate * gravitational constant). Specific impulse is measured in seconds. A propulsion system with a higher Isp uses propellant more efficiently than a system with a lower Isp. If two satellites had the same amount of propellant, but one had a thruster with a high Isp, and the other had a low Isp, the thruster with the high Isp would be able to “burn” (run the engine) for a much longer period of time. Another way to explain Isp is to think of it as how many seconds a given propellant, when paired with a given engine, can accelerate its own initial mass at 1 g.

The Power Equation

Ok, now we know how power, specific impulse (Isp), thrust, and thruster efficiency all relate. However, how do we know if the engine will be able to perform the operation we want our satellite to do? To understand this, we need the rocket equation.

In order to make a maneuver in space, we need to be able to change the velocity of our satellite. For instance, to change from a low earth orbit to a geostationary high earth orbit, your satellite aimed and then accelerated to a speed that can maintain that higher orbit. (See this overly simplistic orbital change depiction below)

The change in velocity (or delta_V) needed to perform any action in the solar system is shown in this “subway map” below. For instance, in the scenario where our satellite is aiming to go to GEO from LEO, it will require 3910 m/s of delta_V (change in velocity). The same concept applies when trying to launch something into space from the earth’s surface – you’ll need 9400 m/s of delta_V to get to orbit.

The Rocket Equation builds on our understanding of the power equation to help us calculate the maximum delta_V our propulsion system can create.
